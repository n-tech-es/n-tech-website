## 3. Target Customer Profile

### Solar ICP
- Texas homeowner (owns, not rents)
- Average monthly electric bill $150–$300+
- Located in Wise, Parker, Jack, or Montague County
- Values transparency and straight answers — skeptical of pushy solar sales
- Practical decision-maker, not an early-adopter idealist
- Has probably been approached by a door-to-door solar company before and didn't like it

### Energy Efficiency ICP
- Homeowner with high utility bills who is unsure whether solar is right yet
- Renters or homeowners wanting actionable efficiency wins without full solar commitment
- Same geographic footprint as solar ICP

## 4. Brand Voice & Tone

**Model after:** Mint Mobile / Ryan Reynolds — dry, confident, slightly self-aware.

**Core principles:**
- Educate first, sell second
- No pushy sales language
- Transparent and straightforward
- Treat the customer like an intelligent adult
- Humor is a tool, not a crutch — use it to disarm, not distract

### Two Approved Humor Styles

**Standard Mint Mobile tone** — dry wit, confident understatement, light sarcasm.
> "Your power company has been charging you rent on electricity you could own. We find that rude."

**Breaking the fourth wall / self-aware** — acknowledges the marketing format itself. Absurdist, meta, slightly deadpan.
> "This is a Facebook post. It's supposed to make you feel something. Here's what we want you to feel: curious about your energy bill."

Mix both styles. Don't default to one exclusively.

### Never Write
- Aggressive CTAs: "ACT NOW! LIMITED TIME!"
- Vague green-washing language: "Save the planet!"
- Fake urgency or manufactured scarcity
- Filler superlatives: "amazing," "incredible," "game-changing"
- Solar-as-prescription framing — always present it as an option ("solar can be a long-term answer," not "solar is the answer")
- Claims about specific savings amounts without qualification
- Stock solar industry copy ("go green," "renewable future," "harness the power of the sun")
- Imply we employ our own installation crews
- Claims about tax incentives/credits without noting they vary by situation

## 8. North Texas-Specific Content Hooks

These angles resonate with the audience and should appear regularly in content:

- **Summer electric bills**: Oncor territory customers routinely pay $250–$400+ in summer. This is the pain point.
- **ERCOT grid reliability**: Texans remember February 2021 (Winter Storm Uri). Energy independence is appealing.
- **Hail / weather context**: North Texas gets hail. Panels are tested to withstand it (address proactively).
- **Rural + suburban mix**: Many customers own larger properties with good roof/land access — favorable for solar.
- **No state income tax but high property tax**: Texans keep an eye on costs — solar as long-term cost control resonates.
- **Oncor net metering**: Relevant for how solar credits work on their specific utility bill.
- **Local installer angle**: "Will Power Electric is based nearby" — community trust signal.
