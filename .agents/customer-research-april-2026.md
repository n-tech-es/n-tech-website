# Customer Research: North Texas Homeowners & Solar
*Compiled: April 2026 | Sources: Facebook community posts (Weatherford, TX), Reddit, KPRC Houston, Texas Appleseed, SolarReviews, Texas AG, EnergySage, Tri-County Electric complaints, Texas Observer, Consumer Affairs*

---

## Raw Data Sources

- **Primary (owned):** 1 Facebook post — Weatherford, Texas Community (Apr 9, 2026), 4 comments
- **Secondary (online):** 40+ data points from Reddit, news investigations, review sites, state AG reports, energy market analysis

## Top Themes (ranked by frequency × intensity)

---

### Theme 1: Bill Volatility and Unpredictability
**Summary:** Electric bills swing dramatically month to month with no obvious cause, leaving homeowners confused and anxious — especially heading into summer.
**Frequency:** 8 of 8 relevant sources
**Intensity:** High — emotional language, specific dollar amounts volunteered unprompted
**Confidence:** High

**Representative quotes:**
- "Mine was $460 last month and $221 this month ... zero changes" — Angela Smith, Weatherford TX Facebook (Apr 2026)
- "Last month was almost 400.00 now it's 126.00 this month" — Yolanda Dilley, Weatherford TX Facebook (Apr 2026)
- "We were paying maybe $350, but in this one year, it's gone from like $350 to some of our bills like being $675." — Central TX homeowner, KCENTV
- "Electric bills jumping from $139 to nearly $800" — multiple Tri-County Electric complaints
- Wholesale ERCOT-North prices projected to increase 45% in 2026 (Energy Ogre market analysis)

**Implications:**
- Bill volatility — not just high bills — is the primary pain. "I don't know what it'll be next month" is as distressing as the number itself.
- Summer is the feared event horizon. Even in April, people are already bracing.
- Leading with "understand your bill before considering solar" hits directly on this pain.

---

### Theme 2: Pre-Emptive Solar Rejection / Entrenched Skepticism
**Summary:** A significant segment of the market rejects solar reflexively, even while actively complaining about bills. The rejection often precedes any real analysis.
**Frequency:** 6 of 8 relevant sources
**Intensity:** High — Tony Dobbs volunteered the rejection in a bill-complaint thread unprompted
**Confidence:** High

**Representative quotes:**
- "And solar is still not the answer" — Tony Dobbs, Weatherford TX Facebook (Apr 2026)
- "It's not worth it to go solar for every Texan" — ConsumerAffairs analysis
- "In most cases solar panels in Texas do not deliver substantial savings" — EnergyBot rural cooperative analysis
- "The hours when electricity has the most value — between 5 and 9 pm — are the same hours when households are using the most power... So instead of exporting power, we were consuming it." — homeowner, solar regret forum

**Implications:**
- This persona has already decided solar isn't for them. Pitching solar directly will confirm their assumption.
- The opening move must be agreement, not persuasion: "You're right — solar isn't always the answer." N-Tech's existing response to Tony Dobbs is textbook correct.
- The diagnostic-first approach is the only credible bridge to this segment.

---

### Theme 3: Deep Distrust of the Solar Industry (Texas-Specific)
**Summary:** Texas has a well-documented solar fraud crisis. Homeowners — especially rural and older ones — have been systematically targeted by predatory door-to-door companies. This creates ambient skepticism that affects every solar conversation in the market.
**Frequency:** 7 of 8 relevant sources
**Intensity:** High — multiple documented cases with named victims, AG investigation, 818% complaint increase
**Confidence:** High

**Representative quotes:**
- "I hope other people will see this and know that they need to be very very careful about purchasing anything, much less the solar systems. The salespeople convinced us with a lie, telling us a government grant would cover the cost." — Bernard Mosley, Houston (KPRC 2)
- "We got the solar panels just to become greener and be more energy efficient but that has been a nightmare... Every time we call we get sent to someone that couldn't help us or hung up." — Jennifer Gulch, Houston (KPRC 2)
- "Loans shifting after 18 months and becoming more expensive" / "liens put on customers' homes" — Texas AG report
- "Door-to-door salespeople usually target the elderly or the low-income, promising they won't pay any electric bill, maybe $50 a month, but when they sign a 32-page contract of legal text, they owe $50,000" — Texas Standard
- "One older person thought they were getting more information on solar panel installation, only to later realize they had signed a contract using a tablet kiosk, locking them into a 30-year contract with hidden fees" — Texas Appleseed

**Statistics:**
- Solar complaints to Texas AG increased 818% (2018–2023)
- 45% of complaints cite false or misleading statements
- 1,000+ formal AG complaints plus thousands more online

**Implications:**
- The Weatherford/North Texas market has been poisoned by bad actors before N-Tech arrived.
- Any homeowner who's seen these news stories (and they have — it's been on local TV) will walk in suspicious.
- N-Tech's "no door-to-door, no commission" positioning isn't a nice-to-have — it's a survival-level differentiator.
- Naming the problem directly ("we know the industry has a trust problem") will build more credibility than avoiding it.

### Theme 4: Utility Lock-In / "No Choice" Resignation
**Summary:** Rural North Texas homeowners often have one utility option — Tri-County Electric or a municipal provider — with no ability to switch. This produces a specific flavor of trapped helplessness that differs from urban deregulated frustration.
**Frequency:** 4 of 8 sources
**Intensity:** Medium-High — "no choice" is the exact phrase used
**Confidence:** High

**Representative quotes:**
- "There's no choice and ours averaged two hundred throughout the winter, and it's below two hundred. Now but we aren't running our heater. Nor our air conditioner so we have made changes." — Barbara Kruger Willkie, Weatherford TX Facebook (Apr 2026)
- "Everyone in the area is complaining of absurd electric bills. We have no other electric companies to change to, forcing us to pay." — Tri-County Electric area customer (PissedConsumer)
- "Are we talking about tri county prices or city of weatherford prices?" — acknowledges that these are meaningfully different situations

**Implications:**
- Tri-County customers can't rate-shop. Solar becomes their only escape valve — but they don't always frame it that way.
- The city of Weatherford vs. Tri-County distinction matters for solar economics (net metering, REP selection). N-Tech needs to address both clearly.
- "Your only way to stop paying whatever they charge" is a compelling but careful frame — don't be hyperbolic.

---

### Theme 5: Behavioral Sacrifice to Manage Bills
**Summary:** Some homeowners have accepted living without comfort (no AC, no heat) as their "solution" to high bills. This is a resigned state, not a solved state.
**Frequency:** 3 sources
**Intensity:** High — involves physical comfort sacrifice
**Confidence:** Medium (limited direct quotes)

**Representative quotes:**
- "We aren't running our heater. Nor our air conditioner so we have made changes." — Barbara Kruger Willkie
- "Staying under $200 without heat or AC takes discipline." — N-Tech reply (validating the sacrifice)
- Average Parker County bill: $167.78/mo; Wise County: $172.08/mo (these averages mask summer spikes of 3-4x)

**Implications:**
- There's a segment who has "solved" the problem by giving up comfort. They don't see themselves as prospects.
- The question "are you running your AC this summer?" reframes their situation — solar can give them permission to be comfortable.
- Don't frame solar as "save money." Frame it as "stop choosing between comfort and affordability."

---

### Theme 6: Net Metering / Oncor Complexity
**Summary:** Texas's deregulated market means solar doesn't work the same way here as in other states. Many homeowners who looked into solar got confused and gave up.
**Frequency:** 4 sources
**Intensity:** Medium
**Confidence:** High

**Representative quotes:**
- "Oncor operates in a deregulated electric market where net metering is not applicable... customers must choose a Retail Electric Provider who offers credits for surplus generation, otherwise solar panels won't be a worthwhile investment." — SolarReviews/Oncor FAQ
- "They cancelled a favorable buyback plan and replaced it with one that would basically zero out my usage but then keep any excess production without compensation." — Texas homeowner (Solar United Neighbors)

**Implications:**
- Confusion about how solar actually works with Oncor/Tri-County is a conversion killer.
- A clear, plain-language explainer ("Here's exactly how solar works with your utility") would directly address this barrier.
- This is a legitimate reason to consult — not a reason to walk away.

---

### Theme 7: Upfront Cost and Affordability Barrier
**Summary:** The cost of solar systems — especially with battery storage — feels out of reach for many homeowners. The expiration of the 30% federal tax credit (Dec 31, 2025) makes this significantly worse.
**Frequency:** 6 sources
**Intensity:** High
**Confidence:** High

**Representative quotes:**
- "The battery is so expensive... installing two batteries would cost $30,000 or more." — Houston homeowner
- "Plano homeowner with a $220 monthly electric bill installed a 12 kW system for $28,000 after incentives. Their new monthly payment is $195, saving $25 monthly immediately" — Texas solar payback example
- Federal ITC expired December 31, 2025 — eliminating 30% cost reduction

**Implications:**
- The ITC expiration is a real objection that can't be dismissed. N-Tech should address it head-on: what changed, what still remains (state/utility incentives), and whether the math still works without it.
- Financing framing ("your payment vs. your bill") matters more than system cost framing.
- The $25/month savings example above shows how thin the margin can be — honest ROI timelines (8-12 years) are critical for trust.

---

### Theme 8: Success Outcomes (What People Want to Feel)
**Summary:** When solar works, customers describe specific emotional and practical relief — not "saving the planet" but "running my fridge during a hurricane" and "bill dropped from $390 to $90."
**Frequency:** 3 sources
**Intensity:** High — described with genuine relief and vindication
**Confidence:** Medium (positive outcomes are underrepresented in online data)

**Representative quotes:**
- "Average electric bill dropped from $390/mo to $90/mo during the hottest summer in 12 years" — Reddit user
- "When a hurricane hits and the world around us is chaos for a few days the last thing I care about is whether the system providing quiet power to our fans, refrigerator, internet router, car charger, and pool pump was a good deal." — Houston homeowner (The Cool Down)
- "The system has saved my a** many times" — off-grid homeowner Reddit review

**Implications:**
- Outcome language should be specific and physical: fans, fridge, AC, lights — not "energy independence."
- Grid resilience is an underutilized angle in North Texas messaging (post-Uri trauma is still real).
- Summer bill relief is the primary desired outcome — not environmental impact, not ROI.

## Personas

---

### Persona 1: "The Resigned Payor" — Barbara

**Profile**
- Age: 55–75, homeowner in rural/suburban North TX
- Utility: Tri-County Electric or municipal cooperative — no ability to switch providers
- Household: Long-time homeowner, possibly retired or near-retirement

**Primary Job to Be Done**
Get through summer without financial or physical hardship.

**Trigger Events**
- Bills averaging $200+ in winter while running no HVAC
- Dread of what summer will bring with AC running

**Top Pains**
1. "There's no choice" — feeling structurally trapped by utility
2. Sacrificing comfort (no heat, no AC) just to manage the bill
3. Doesn't understand why the bill swings so much

**Desired Outcomes**
- Run the AC in summer without financial anxiety
- Have a predictable bill she can plan around

**Objections and Fears**
- Has probably been approached by door-to-door solar before and dismissed it
- Worries about being scammed or signing something she doesn't understand
- Doesn't know if solar works with her utility

**Alternatives Considered**
- Behavioral sacrifice (already doing this)
- Doing nothing and accepting the bill

**Key Vocabulary**
- "No choice"
- "We've made changes"
- "Tri-County" (says it as a known entity with its own rules)

**How to Reach Her**
- Facebook community groups (already engaged here)
- Neighbors/word of mouth
- Local community events

**Alternatives Considered**
- Doing nothing
- Energy efficiency improvements
- Rate shopping (if on Oncor)

**Key Vocabulary**
- "Not the answer"
- "Usually a reason behind that"
- "I know"

**How to Reach Him**
- He won't respond to ads; he responds to conversations
- The Facebook community post model is ideal — N-Tech as a helpful resource, not a salesperson
- Peer recommendations from people he trusts

## Trigger Events (What Starts the Journey)

1. **Summer bill spike** — the biggest trigger in North TX. Bills going from $150 in spring to $450+ in July/August.
2. **Unexplained bill swing** — "zero changes" but bill doubled. Creates urgency and confusion simultaneously.
3. **Utility rate increase announcement** — ERCOT wholesale +45% in 2026 will generate search activity.
4. **Neighbor gets solar** — word-of-mouth from a trusted person who had a good experience.
5. **Grid outage** — post-Winter Storm Uri fear is still present; any outage event reactivates it.
6. **Sacrifice fatigue** — living without AC for another summer becomes unacceptable.

## Messaging Implications

1. **Lead with the bill problem, not solar.** "How's your electric bill doing?" outperforms any solar ad. N-Tech already does this — double down.

2. **The fraud context is N-Tech's biggest competitive opening.** Every homeowner in North TX has either been pitched aggressively or heard the horror stories. Being explicitly different from that — no door-to-door, no commission, no pressure, local — is not just a feature, it's the brand.

3. **"Solar can be an option" is correct positioning.** Tony Dobbs can't be sold — but he can be respected. N-Tech's response to him was the right move. That brand voice should be consistent across all site copy.

4. **Summer anxiety is the hook, not ROI.** "Stop choosing between comfort and affordability every summer" lands harder than payback-period math.

5. **Tri-County vs. Oncor matters.** These are meaningfully different situations. A page or section that speaks to each specifically (how solar works with your provider) would eliminate a major confusion barrier.

6. **Address the ITC expiration honestly.** It's a real objection, and pretending it didn't happen damages trust. Acknowledge it, explain what changed, and explain why (or when) it still makes sense.
