The 30% Federal Solar Tax Credit Is Gone. Here's the Honest Answer for North Texas Homeowners.

The credit expired December 31, 2025. If you've been putting off a solar decision waiting to figure out the incentives, here's what actually changed — and what it means for the math in North Texas.

Home > Blog > The Clock Is Ticking on the Solar Tax Credit

A lot of North Texas homeowners were waiting to get the solar numbers until they figured out the tax credit situation. Here's the situation: the 30% federal credit expired December 31, 2025. It's not threatened — it's gone. This page explains what that actually means for the math, what incentives still exist in Texas, and whether solar can still make sense without it.

The Short Version

The federal 30% solar tax credit no longer applies to new installations. If you install solar in 2026, you cannot claim it. The math changed — the payback period is longer without it. Whether solar still makes sense for your home depends on your utility rate, your usage, and where rates are headed. We'll give you the real numbers.

What Was the Federal Solar Tax Credit?

The Investment Tax Credit — the ITC — was a federal incentive that let homeowners deduct 30% of their total solar installation cost directly from their federal income tax bill. It was not a rebate or a discount — it was a dollar-for-dollar reduction in what you owed the IRS.

Here's what it looked like on a typical North Texas installation:

System Cost 30% Credit (no longer available) Net Cost With Credit Cost Without Credit (2026)
$17,000$5,100$11,900$17,000
$20,000$6,000$14,000$20,000
$25,000$7,500$17,500$25,000
$30,000$9,000$21,000$30,000

The ITC was extended through 2032 by the Inflation Reduction Act in 2022, but subsequent Congressional action eliminated it effective December 31, 2025. Homeowners who installed solar before that date can still claim the credit on their taxes. Anyone installing now cannot.

What Texas Incentives Are Still Available?

The federal credit is gone, but two meaningful Texas-specific incentives remain:

  • Sales tax exemption. Texas exempts solar equipment from state sales tax. At the standard North Texas rate of roughly 8.25%, that's a savings of $1,400–$2,500 on a typical system — not nothing.
  • Property tax homestead exemption. Under Texas law, the added value a solar installation brings to your home is excluded from property tax reassessment. If solar adds $15,000 to your home's value, your property tax bill doesn't go up because of it.

Texas also has no state income tax, which means there was never a state solar credit to stack on top of the federal one — and there still isn't. The two exemptions above are what's available.

What This Means in Real Dollars

On a $22,000 system, the sales tax exemption saves roughly $1,800 at closing. The property tax benefit compounds over time — if solar adds $15,000 in home value, you avoid roughly $300–$400 per year in property taxes depending on your county rate. That's real money, just not the same scale as the old 30% credit.

Does Solar Still Make Sense Without the Credit?

Honest answer: it depends on your situation. The math got harder — there's no way around that. A system that had a 9-year payback with the 30% credit might have a 13-year payback without it. That's a meaningful difference, and we won't pretend otherwise.

But there are factors working in the other direction:

  • Electricity rates in North Texas are rising. ERCOT wholesale prices at the North hub are projected to increase significantly in 2026 due to data center demand and grid strain. Higher utility rates mean faster payback on a solar investment.
  • If you're on Tri-County Electric or a similar cooperative, you have no rate options. Whatever they charge, you pay. Solar can be the only way to reduce that exposure.
  • Summer bills in Wise and Parker County regularly hit $400–$600+. A system designed around your peak usage can meaningfully change that — even if the payback is longer than it was a year ago.
  • Solar locks in your cost of generation. Once the system is paid off, you're generating electricity at close to zero marginal cost — regardless of what the utility is charging at that point.

For some households, the numbers still work clearly. For others, they don't — and we'll tell you that honestly. What we won't do is pressure anyone into a decision that doesn't hold up mathematically.

A Note on the Situation in North Texas Specifically

Most of the solar content you'll find online is written for California, the Northeast, or states with robust state-level incentives. North Texas is a different situation:

  • No state credit, but no state income tax either
  • Oncor territory has a real interconnection process — not instantaneous, but manageable
  • Many rural homeowners are on cooperative utilities with no ability to switch providers
  • Summer bills are the primary financial driver, not average annual usage

The right analysis for a homeowner in Decatur or Weatherford looks different from someone in Austin or Houston. We build proposals based on your actual usage, your utility, and your roof — not national averages.

We'll Tell You if It Doesn't Work for You

Solar isn't always the answer. A lot of companies in this industry won't say that — we will. There are households where the payback period is long enough that solar doesn't make sense right now. There are roofs that aren't suitable. There are situations where improving insulation or addressing HVAC efficiency first would do more for your bill than panels.

You'll see what a system would cost at current pricing, what your estimated savings are based on your actual usage, and what your payback looks like without the federal credit. If it works, great.
