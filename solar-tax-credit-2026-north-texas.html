The Solar Tax Credit Is Gone. Here's What North Texas Homeowners Need to Know Now.

The federal 30% solar tax credit expired December 31, 2025. The numbers have changed. Here's an honest look at what incentives remain and whether solar still makes sense in North Texas.

Home > Blog > The Solar Tax Credit Is Gone

The Texas solar sales tax exemption — the one most North Texas homeowners were counting on — expired December 31, 2025. If you've been planning your solar purchase around that credit, the math has changed. This is an honest look at what incentives remain, what the real numbers look like without the ITC, and whether solar can still make sense in North Texas in 2026.

The Short Version

The federal ITC is gone. Two Texas-specific incentives remain: a sales tax exemption on solar equipment and a property tax exemption on added home value. These are real but smaller than the ITC was. Payback periods are longer. For many homeowners the numbers can still work — just differently than before.

What Happened to the Federal Tax Credit

The federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC) was extended through 2032 under the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022. It didn't make it that far. Congress allowed the residential solar ITC to expire at the end of 2025. The 30% credit — which reduced a $24,000 system to roughly $16,800 — is no longer available to homeowners installing solar in 2026.

This was the single largest financial incentive for residential solar in the U.S. Its expiration is significant, and any installer who isn't acknowledging that plainly is not being straight with you.

What Incentives Still Exist in Texas

Two state-level benefits survived the ITC's expiration:

1. Texas Solar Equipment Sales Tax Exemption

Solar panels, inverters, racking, and related equipment are exempt from Texas state sales tax (8.25%). On the equipment portion of a typical residential system, this saves $1,500–$2,000 depending on system size. It's not the ITC, but it's real money and it applies automatically — you don't have to claim it on a tax return.

2. Texas Homestead Property Tax Exemption for Solar

Under Texas law, the added value a solar system brings to your home's appraised value is exempt from property taxes. If your home gains $15,000–$20,000 in value from a solar installation, you don't pay property taxes on that increase for as long as you own the home. At North Texas property tax rates of 2–2.5%, that's $300–$500 per year in ongoing savings — a real benefit that compounds over time.

Texas Has No State Income Tax

There is no state-level solar tax credit in Texas, and there never was — Texas doesn't have a state income tax. The federal ITC was the only tax credit available to Texas homeowners. What remains are the exemptions above, plus the long-term electricity bill savings from the system itself.

Does Solar Still Make Sense in North Texas?

The honest answer is: it depends on your situation — more so now than before. Without the ITC, the investment is larger upfront and the payback period is longer. Here's how to think through it:

  • Time horizon matters more now. A system that previously paid back in 7 years might now take 9–12. If you plan to stay in the home for 15+ years, the long-term savings can still be meaningful. If you're likely to move in 5 years, the math is harder.
  • Your electricity bill is the main variable. High-usage households — 1,500 kWh/month or more — see larger monthly offsets and shorter payback periods. If your bills are already low, the case is weaker.
  • Electricity rates are still rising. Every rate increase that happens while you're unprotected adds to the cost of waiting. A solar system locks in a portion of your energy cost for 25+ years.
  • Battery storage has value independent of the ITC. If outage protection is part of your reasoning, that benefit doesn't disappear with the credit. Texas weather events aren't getting less severe.
  • Roof condition and shading are still the fundamentals. A south-facing roof with minimal shading in a high-electricity-use home is still a strong solar candidate — the economics just need to be evaluated honestly against current numbers.
